What's This All About?

As both an engineer and a cyclist, I got tired of the one-size-fits-all approach to aerobars. After looking at what the pros use - custom-fabricated aerobars perfectly tailored to their position - I thought, why not make my own? With CAD skills, a 3D printer, and some aluminum stock, I could replicate those high-end setups for a fraction of the cost.

The goal: create aerobars that fit my exact riding position, look professional, and perform like the custom setups you see at Ironman World Championships. This meant ditching the clunky adjustable systems and going for a clean, integrated design - aluminum frame with 3D printed forearm cups, mounted directly to the stem. No compromises, just custom.

The Challenge

Every cyclist is built differently, but most aerobars are either cheap and uncomfortable or crazy expensive. The high-end custom setups that pros use? Those will set you back $1000+ and require sending your bike to a specialist. The challenge was to replicate that level of custom fit and performance without the massive price tag.

I needed aerobars that would:

  • Perfectly match my riding position (no adjustability needed if it's designed right)
  • Support my entire forearms comfortably for long-distance riding
  • Mount cleanly to my stem for that integrated, pro look
  • Be strong enough to handle hard efforts without flexing
  • Look like they belong on the bike, not like an aftermarket add-on

Design & Development Process

1. Research & Position Mapping

Started by studying how pro triathletes and time trialists set up their positions. Took detailed measurements of my optimal aero position - reach, stack, elbow width, forearm angle. I spent hours on the bike with tape and cardboard mockups to nail down exactly where my arms needed to be. Let's just say there was a previous attempt that... didn't go great. Learned a lot from that one! üòÖ

2. CAD Modeling - The Hybrid Approach

Fired up Fusion 360 and designed a hybrid system combining the best of both worlds:

  • Aluminum frame: Main structure for strength and stiffness - this takes all the load
  • Stem mount: Custom aluminum clamp that replaces the standard stem cap, creating a clean integrated look
  • 3D printed forearm cups: Ergonomic supports molded exactly to my forearm shape for maximum comfort
  • No adjustability: Everything dimensioned precisely for my position (if it's right the first time, you don't need to adjust)

The design went through several iterations. Modeling the forearm cups to provide full support while keeping weight down was tricky - had to balance comfort with minimal material. The stem mount needed to be bomb-proof since that's the critical structural connection.

3. Structural Analysis

Before fabricating anything, I ran FEA simulations on the aluminum frame to verify it could handle the loads. Modeled worst-case scenarios - full body weight on the forearms during a sprint, hitting bumps, lateral forces from handling. The stem mount got extra attention since that's the single point of attachment.

The beauty of using aluminum for the main structure is that I could design it thin and light while still being incredibly stiff. The 3D printed cups don't carry structural loads - they're just there for comfort and support.

Fabrication & Assembly

Aluminum Frame & Mount

The aluminum components were fabricated from 6061-T6 aluminum stock. The main frame is simple but effective - straight sections with carefully positioned bends to match my riding geometry. The stem mount was the trickiest part - it replaces the standard stem clamp and had to fit precisely while maintaining clamping force.

  • Cut and bent aluminum tubing to exact dimensions
  • Machined the stem mount interface for perfect fit
  • Deburred all edges (aluminum splinters are no joke)
  • Anodized black to match the bike aesthetic

3D Printed Forearm Cups

The forearm cups were 3D printed in PETG (durable and slightly flexible). These are shaped to cradle my entire forearms, distributing pressure evenly instead of just having small pads like traditional aerobars. Key printing decisions:

  • Printed at 0.2mm layer height for smooth surface finish
  • Used organic supports to maintain the complex curved geometry
  • 40% infill was plenty - these don't carry structural loads
  • Post-processed with acetone vapor for a smoother finish
  • Added foam padding on the inside for extra comfort

Testing & Real-World Performance

Static Testing

Before trusting these on an actual ride, I loaded them up in a bench test to simulate riding forces. Put my full body weight on the forearm cups, tested the stem mount under various loads, checked for any flex or movement. The aluminum frame is rock solid - zero flex even under hard loads.

What I Learned

This project taught me a ton about product design and manufacturing:

  • Full forearm support is massively more comfortable than traditional arm pads
  • Getting the position right takes time and patience - mockups are your friend
  • Hybrid designs (metal + 3D printing) leverage the strengths of both materials
  • Sometimes the first version teaches you what NOT to do (that's valuable too!)
  • Custom fabrication lets you replicate pro-level equipment on a DIY budget
