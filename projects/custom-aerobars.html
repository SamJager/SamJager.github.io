What's This All About?

As both an engineer and a triathlete, I wasn't happy with the aerobar options on the market. Commercial options either cost a fortune or use small pads that aren't very comfortable. Inspired by custom setups with full arm coverage like the ones used by Magnus Ditlev and other pro triathletes, I decided to create a solution specific to my position, bike, and components.

The goal was simple: create custom aerobars that fit my exact riding position, are adjustable for fine-tuning, and look way cooler than off-the-shelf options. Plus, I wanted to prove that with modern CAD tools and 3D printing, custom bike components are totally achievable.

Tools & Technologies Used

Fusion 360 FEA Analysis 3D Printing (FDM) Carbon Fiber Layup Digital Calipers

The Challenge

Every cyclist is built differently, but most aerobars come in just a few sizes. Finding the perfect fit usually means:

  • Spending $500+ on high-end adjustable systems (ouch, my wallet)
  • Compromising on fit and dealing with discomfort on long rides
  • Buying multiple spacers, extenders, and adapters that add weight and look messy
  • Still not getting exactly what you want

My bike needed aerobars that would work with my specific geometry, allow for easy adjustments, be strong enough to handle the forces during hard efforts, and - let's be honest - look sick.

Design & Development Process

1. Research & Measurements

Started by measuring my current riding position and researching bike fit principles. I took photos and videos of my position on the bike, measured reach and stack requirements, and studied how commercial aerobars are designed. Turns out, there's a lot of biomechanics involved in getting this right!

2. CAD Modeling

Fired up Fusion 360 and got to work. The design needed to:

  • Clamp securely to standard handlebars without damaging them
  • Provide adjustable arm pad positioning (because bike fit is never "done")
  • Include comfortable grip extensions at the right angle
  • Keep the weight reasonable (every gram counts, right?)
  • Look sleek and aerodynamic (form follows function, but it should look good too)

I went through probably a dozen iterations, tweaking angles, adjusting lengths, and optimizing the internal structure for strength while minimizing material use.

3. Structural Analysis

Before printing anything, I ran FEA simulations to make sure these wouldn't snap during a hard sprint. Modeled realistic loading conditions (body weight + dynamic loads from riding) and checked stress concentrations at critical points like the clamp interface and arm pad mounts.

The first design? Way too flexible. The second? Overbuilt and heavy. Third time's the charm - found the sweet spot with internal ribbing that keeps it stiff without adding unnecessary weight.

Manufacturing & Assembly

3D Printing Strategy

Printed the main components using PETG (tougher than PLA, easier than nylon). Key printing decisions:

  • 100% infill in high-stress areas like clamps and mounting points
  • Oriented parts to align layer lines with load directions (strength > aesthetics... but both are nice)
  • Printed at 0.2mm layer height for a balance of strength and print time
  • Added extra perimeters (walls) for impact resistance

Post-Processing & Assembly

After printing, I cleaned up the parts, test-fit everything, and made some refinements:

  • Sanded contact surfaces for a smooth finish
  • Added rubber padding in the clamp area to protect the handlebars
  • Wrapped the arm pads with foam and bar tape for comfort
  • Used stainless steel hardware throughout (because rusty bolts on a bike are unacceptable)

Testing & Real-World Performance

Static Testing

Before trusting these on an actual ride, I clamped them in a vice and loaded them up to simulate riding forces. Applied weight gradually while watching for any flex, creaking, or movement. They held up great - no slipping, no cracking, solid as a rock.

What I Learned

This project taught me a ton about product design and manufacturing:

  • Ergonomics matter way more than I thought - small changes make huge differences
  • FEA is super useful, but nothing beats real-world testing
  • Designing for 3D printing requires different thinking than traditional manufacturing
  • Iteration is key - my first design was... not great. That's okay!
  • Custom fabrication is incredibly empowering - if you need it, you can build it
