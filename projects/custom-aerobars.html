What's This All About?

As both an engineer and a cyclist, I got tired of the one-size-fits-all approach to aerobars. Inspired by the one piece bars used by pro triathletes like Magnus Ditlev, I decided to make my own as an engineer With CAD skills, a 3D printer, aluminum stock, and access to capable tools, I can create a similar solution to those high-end setups for a fraction of the cost.

The goal: create aerobars that fit my exact riding position, look professional, and perform like the custom setups you see at Ironman World Championships. This meant ditching the clunky adjustable systems and going for a clean, integrated design - aluminum frame with 3D printed forearm cups, mounted directly to the stem. No compromises, just custom.

Tools & Technologies Used

Onshape 3D Printing (ASA-CF) Aluminum Fabrication CNC/Manual Machining

The Challenge

Every cyclist is built differently, but most aerobars are either cheap and uncomfortable or crazy expensive. The high-end custom setups that pros use? Those will set you back $1000+ and require sending your bike to a specialist. The challenge was to replicate that level of custom fit and performance without the massive price tag.

I needed aerobars that would:

  • Perfectly match my riding position (no adjustability needed if it's designed right)
  • Support my entire forearms comfortably for long-distance riding
  • Mount cleanly to my stem for that integrated, pro look
  • Be strong enough to handle hard efforts and bumps with minimal flexing
  • Look like they belong on the bike, not like a homemade solution

Design & Development Process

1. Research & Position Mapping

As a starting point I look and level of polish to match. I also had a position I already found works for me through use of a basic set of aerobars, and a previous version that... didn't meet my standards üòÖ. But I learned a lot from it!

2. CAD Modeling - The Hybrid Approach

Fired up Onshape and designed a hybrid system combining the best of both worlds:

  • Aluminum frame: Main structure for strength and stiffness - this takes all the load
  • Stem mount: Custom aluminum clamp that replaces the standard stem cap, creating a clean integrated look
  • 3D printed forearm cups: Ergonomic supports molded exactly to my forearm shape for maximum comfort
  • No adjustability: Everything dimensioned precisely for my position (if it's right the first time, you don't need to adjust)

Fabrication & Assembly

Aluminum Frame & Mount

The aluminum structural components were fabricated from aluminum stock. The main frame is simple but effective - the profile was cut on a waterjet and tapped holes added on a bridgeport. The stem mount was a bit more difficult - it was also cut in profile on a waterjet, then some milling, drilling, and counterboring made for a drop in replacement for the existing stem clamp.

3D Printed Forearm Cups

The forearm cups were 3D printed in ASA-CF for UV resistance and stiffness. These are shaped to cradle my entire forearms, distributing pressure evenly instead of just having small pads like traditional aerobars. Key printing decisions:

  • Printed at 0.2mm layer height for smooth surface finish
  • Split into 2 parts to fit on a Bambu X1 printer
  • just a few perimeters and low infill kept weight down, the shape makes it strong
  • Added foam padding on the inside for extra comfort

Testing & Real-World Performance

Static Testing

Before trusting these on an actual ride, I loaded them up in a bench test to simulate riding forces. Put significantly more force than would rotate the bars on basic bar clamp aerobars and the bars felt great. A bit of flex that absorbs some impact from bumps, but not enough that if feels concerning.

On-Bike Testing

Time for the moment of truth - actually riding with them! Started cautious but quickly gained confidence. The difference from my previous attempt was night and day.
