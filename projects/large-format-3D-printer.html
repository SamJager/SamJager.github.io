High Temp, Large Format Custom 3D Printer

Inspired by open source printers but finding them lacking, I decided to build my own.

Date: 2024–Present Category: CAD Design / Fabrication / Software Tools: Onshape, Waterjet
← Back to Projects

Project Overview

I have always loved FFF 3D printing since I first learned about it. Making complex three-dimensional shapes with basic CAD skills and a machine that costs well under $1,000? What's not to love? Backed by an incredible open source community, great software, and endless material options, it is an absolutely incredible resource.

While I love the extensive existing 3D printer options (and they have improved greatly since I started this project), they still leave me wanting more. The consumer 3D printer market is only now starting to offer printers with chamber temperatures acceptable for printing basic materials like ABS and Nylon, and no one even seems to be trying to reach true engineering materials outside of industrial printers. Additionally, the anisotropy from poor layer adhesion could be mitigated by non-planar or 5-axis printing.

The plan I arrived at was to build a fully custom printer with a bomb-proof metal motion system capable of handling true engineering materials should I choose to use a compatible extrusion system. I also decided to use three independent ball screw-driven Z axes, creating 2-axis bed tilt capability for my dreams of attempting 5-axis FFF printing. While I was at it, I might as well make it big—big printers are fun, and 5-axis printing would greatly benefit from a larger platform.

Problem Statement

As I started this project, I quickly developed a list of requirements and desired features.

Requirements:

  • All core motion system components capable of operating at >100°C
  • Bomb-proof motion system allowing for future ambitious projects
  • Upgrade potential for 5-axis printing (2-axis bed tilt)

Desired Features:

  • Large format >300mm in all axes
  • Multi-toolhead for support material and multi-material printing
  • High-speed printing

Design & Development

Given the wide range of successful FFF printers out there, it would be foolish to start from scratch. My inspiration began with the Voron Trident for its effective CoreXY motion system and three Z axes enabling 2-axis bed tilt. I was also inspired by the E3D Toolchanger and Pantheon printers, which use a large flat plate as a consistent XY motion plane. While I didn't want to develop my own toolchanger, the Prusa XL inspired me to leave a large space at the back for docking toolheads.

Key Design Points

T-Slot Frame

1/5" T-slot aluminum extrusion frame ensures rigidity while providing easy mounting locations for the high-temperature-rated insulation required to achieve the enclosure temperatures needed for true engineering materials.

Image Placeholder — Frame Assembly

XY Motion System

The XY motion system is built on a 1/2" MIC6 aluminum tooling plate, serving as a robust and flat platform. The X axis gantry was originally planned as a T-slot extrusion, but it didn't inspire confidence in rigidity, so I switched to a stacked plate construction.

Image Placeholder — XY Gantry / MIC6 Plate

Triple Ball Screw Z Axis

Three independent ball screw-driven Z axes provide the platform for 2-axis bed tilt and are built for continuous bed motion, enabling future 5-axis printing capability.

Image Placeholder — Z-Axis Assembly

400mm MIC6 Bed

A 400mm MIC6 aluminum tooling plate combined with a fiberglass build plate ensures flatness, minimizes warping when heated, and prevents the build plate peeling seen on large parts printed on magnetic build plates.
